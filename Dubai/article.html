From London to Dubai: Our Adventure in the City of Gold

A British-American couple's journey into the heart of the UAE
By Sarah Thompson & Mike Johnson | October 28, 2024

Where tradition meets tomorrow – sometimes at the same traffic light

When Mike first suggested moving to Dubai, I thought he'd gone completely barmyBritish slang for foolish or crazy. There we were, comfortable in our London flat, me with my British sensibilities and him with his American optimism, contemplating a move to the desert. But two years later, I can tell you that diving into the unknown was the best decision we've ever made.

"The moment you land in Dubai, you realize you're not just in another city – you're in tomorrow. The skyline looks like something from a sci-fi film, but the heart of the city beats with ancient rhythms."

Our first encounter with Dubai's juxtapositionThe fact of two things being placed close together with contrasting effect of old and new came during our house hunting adventure. We found ourselves choosing between an ultra-modern apartment in the megalithicResembling or characterized by huge proportions Dubai Marina and a charming villa in Al Wasl, where the call to prayer mingles with children playing in the streets.

💡 Did You Know?

Dubai's rapid transformation from a fishing village to a global metropolis happened in just over 50 years. The discovery of oil in 1966 catalyzed this remarkable metamorphosis, though today oil accounts for less than 1% of Dubai's GDP!

Daily Life in the Desert Metropolis

Life here operates on what we've come to call "Dubai Time." It's a curious blend of fast-paced business and languorousCharacterized by laziness or lack of energy leisure. While the city never sleeps, everything moves at its own peculiar rhythm. Business meetings might start at 10 PM, and dinner reservations rarely exist before 9 PM.

A Day in Our Dubai Life

6:30 AM: Wake up to the harmonious blend of the call to prayer and construction cranes (Dubai's unofficial anthem).
7:30 AM: Navigate the metro's Gold Class cabin, where you'll hear at least six languages before reaching your stop. The morning commute is a fashion show of global proportions.
10:00 AM: First meeting of the day, where "five minutes" could mean anything from five actual minutes to an hour – we call it "Dubai Standard Time."
1:00 PM: Lunch could be anything from a 3 AED shawarmaA Middle Eastern dish of meat cut into thin slices and served in pita bread to a business lunch in a rotating rooftop restaurant.
4:00 PM: Duck into an air-conditioned mall to escape the afternoon heat. Shopping isn't just a pastime here; it's a survival strategy.
7:00 PM: Evening adventures begin - perhaps a desert safari or a yacht dinner cruise. The city transforms as the sun sets.
11:00 PM: Still wide awake! The city's nightlife is just warming up, and somewhere, a business deal is being sealed over Arabic coffee and dates.

Mike's direct American bargaining style needed some British diplomatic finessing. Now we've mastered the art of tea-drinking and price-haggling, turning shopping into a social event rather than a transaction.

"In Dubai, everything is a conversation. Whether you're buying spices or signing a business deal, relationships matter more than transactions. It's not about time, it's about trust."

Let me tell you about our everyday adventures that make Dubai unique. Take our morning commute: Mike once found himself in a traffic jam behind a convoy of supercars, two camels (yes, actual camels!), and a golf cart - only in Dubai! The kerfuffleA commotion or fuss, especially one caused by conflicting views that ensued when everyone tried to merge into one lane was both hilarious and uniquely Dubai.

🔑 Survival Tips for New Expats

  • Always have your location pin ready to share on WhatsApp - it's the unofficial second language of Dubai delivery services!
  • Learn to dress for multiple occasions in one day - you might go from a conservative business meeting to a beach club
  • Get comfortable with "Inshallah time" - things happen when they happen
  • Master the art of mall navigation - they're your climate-controlled lifeline in summer

The summer heat was our biggest challenge. When temperatures soar to swelteringUncomfortably hot; oppressively humid heights of 45°C (113°F), you learn to appreciate the indoor city that Dubai has created. From ski slopes to aquariums in shopping malls, life continues in climate-controlled splendor.

Cultural Collisions and Discoveries

You haven't truly experienced Dubai until you've had at least three cultural misunderstandings before breakfast. Like the time I tried to shake hands with a conservative businessman (note: don't), or when Mike enthusiastically gave a thumbs-up in a meeting (another cultural faux pas we quickly learned about). These moments of multifariousMany and of various types cultural exchange have become our favorite dinner party stories.

💡 Daily Life Quirks

In Dubai, you'll find yourself giving directions using landmarks rather than street names. "It's near the building that looks like a rolled-up newspaper, opposite the golden shopping cart sculpture" is a perfectly normal way to explain where you live! And yes, that building actually exists.

Our social circle has become a potpourriA mixture of diverse things of nationalities. Our dinner parties now feature British roasts alongside American pies, Arabic mezze, and Indian curries. Dubai's cosmopolitanIncluding people from many different countries nature means you're never just learning one new culture – you're experiencing dozens.

The Great Address Adventure

Speaking of addresses, ordering delivery here is an adventure in itself. Our first attempt to get food delivered went something like this:

Delivery Driver: "I'm near you."
Us: "Great! We're in the blue building."
Driver: "There are 7 blue buildings."
Us: "The one with the..." *spends 15 minutes describing every architectural feature while our food gets cold*

But that's part of Dubai's charm. The city operates on a system of organized chaos that somehow works perfectly. We've learned to embrace the uncertainty and go with the flow, whether it's dealing with bureaucratic procedures that change weekly or navigating the complex social etiquette of when to kiss, bow, or shake hands (or not).

The dichotomyA division or contrast between two things between tradition and modernity is ever-present. One morning you might find yourself in a self-driving Tesla headed to a blockchain conference, and by evening you're riding a wooden abraA traditional wooden boat used to ferry passengers across Dubai Creek across the Creek to eat at a family-owned restaurant that's lasted generations.

Perhaps the most surprising aspect has been watching how Dubai amalgamatesCombines or unites to form one organization or structure seemingly contradictory elements into a harmonious whole. Modern women in business suits walk alongside those in traditional abayas, ancient desert traditions coexist with cryptocurrency innovations, and somehow it all works.

"Dubai doesn't ask you to choose between old and new, East and West, tradition and innovation. Instead, it asks you to embrace all of it, creating something entirely unique in the process."

One of our most memorable moments was during our first Ramadan, when we inadvertentlyUnintentionally; by accident found ourselves in a spontaneous iftar (breaking of the fast) with our neighbors. What started as borrowing some sugar turned into a feast that lasted until midnight, complete with life stories shared in a mixture of broken English, Arabic, and enthusiastic hand gestures.

Looking Back, Looking Forward

Two years have passed since we made that leap of faith, and Dubai has transformed us as much as it continues to transform itself. We've learned to measure time not by the clock but by the rhythm of calls to prayer, to find our way using the Burj Khalifa as our North Star, and to understand that "yes" often means "maybe," and "inshallah" can mean anything from "definitely" to "not a chance."

🔑 Final Thoughts

Moving to Dubai requires adaptability, openness to new experiences, and a willingness to embrace contradiction. It's not just about relocating to a new city – it's about adopting a new way of seeing the world. As we often tell our friends back home: Dubai doesn't just change your address; it changes your perspective.

🏢 Board Meeting at DIFC Highly Formal
CEO: "I trust you've had a chance to review the quarterly projections?"
Sarah: "Indeed, I have. If I may, there are several key metrics that warrant our attention. The ROI on the new initiative has exceeded our initial estimates by a considerable margin."
Board Member: "That's rather encouraging. Though I must say, I have some reservations about the timeline. Might we not be better served by adopting a more measured approach?"
Mike: "While I appreciate your concerns, our market analysis suggests that timing is absolutely crucial here. We're looking at a prime window of opportunity."
Sarah: "If I might add to that - we've run extensive sensitivity analyses. Even in our most conservative scenarios, the risk-reward ratio remains quite favorable."

🔍 Language Notes:

  • "If I may" = polite way to offer an opinion
  • "Warrant our attention" = deserve to be considered (formal)
  • "Rather encouraging" = very positive (understated British style)
  • "Might we not be" = very formal way to make a suggestion
  • "If I might add" = polite way to contribute to discussion
